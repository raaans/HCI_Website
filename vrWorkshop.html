Meshes & Materials

First, we learned a bit about projects and the interface of the Unreal Editor. That's not too interesting to go into here, so I'll skip towards the first real lesson: meshes and materials.

A mesh is, simply put, the shape of a... thing. This thing doesn't have a specific colour or texture or anything else. That's it!

The surface of this 'thing' is made up of it's material properties. The material determines the base colour and how metallic, shiny or rough our thing is. Add in the texture (a simple 2D image that's pasted on top of the mesh), and you have completed your thing!

Blueprints

A blueprint is something even more abstract than the thing we just did. Whereas a mesh is the shape and a material is the surface of that shape, a blueprint can be best described as a behaviour: Something that a thing does. Or something. Are you still following? Because I barely am.

In the meshes and materials section I skipped over the use of nodes, so I'll address it here. A node contains the properties of the thing you're making. So your material would have a 'colour' node, a 'specularity' node, etc. Blueprints work similarly, in that they have nodes for every action that happens.
